Life Chapters

2020–2021Pandemic Knowledge Binge
March 2020. Nowhere to go. Kurzgesagt, Veritasium and Philosophy Tube became the curriculum nobody signed up for. The algorithm delivered weeks of cosmology, epistemology and explainer videos on things I'd never thought to wonder about. Twelve-minute videos on black dwarfs. Forty-minute essays on free will. The pandemic was terrible but the YouTube backlog was genuinely educational.
2021–2022Analog Sound Rabbit Hole
Started with a Nina Simone live concert on YouTube. Ended three months later deep in vinyl restoration tutorials, Hi-Fi amplifier physics, and My Analog Journal. Bought a turntable in November 2021. Now maintain an opinion about moving coil cartridges. This is a YouTube-specific danger.
2022–2023Film Language Phase
Every Frame a Painting archives in one sitting. Then scene-by-scene breakdowns of Villeneuve, Tarkovsky, and Kubrick. Tom Scott's editing style analysis. Fell into video essays about cinematography, sound design, and what directors are actually saying when they choose a lens or an angle. Cinema stopped being passive.
2023–2024Architecture & The City
Tom Scott videos about weird UK infrastructure led to architecture channels. Brutalism, Bauhaus, Berlin housing estates, the urban planning failures of the 70s. Somehow ended up watching six hours of Jane Jacobs content. Philosophy Tube's gentrification essay remains one of the best things I've watched.

Patterns
I am one.

Your taste converges on a single obsession: serious ideas rendered in beautiful form. Whether it's hard SF that takes evolutionary biology as seriously as Tchaikovsky does, slow cinema that trusts the audience to sit with unease, or jazz that turns sadness into architecture — you want work that believes in itself. You rate 5 stars rarely and precisely. The canon you're building is characterised by moral complexity, patient pacing, and a preference for ambiguity over resolution. You consume across every medium but with consistent depth rather than breadth — finishing series, re-reading novels, returning to albums. The recurring motif across your highest-rated work is the outsider who sees clearly because they're not invested in the system's self-image.

Core Themes

  • Identity and transformation under pressure
  • Moral ambiguity in systems of power
  • Memory, loss, and the weight of the past
  • Survival and humanity at civilisational scale
  • The outsider who sees what insiders cannot

What I Don't Like

  • Formulaic genre fiction without thematic ambition (airport thrillers, most superhero films after 2015)
  • Comedy that relies on embarrassment or cruelty rather than wit
  • Exposition-heavy storytelling that explains what it should show
  • High-concept SF that uses ideas as decoration rather than investigation

Taste Clusters

Hard SF & Speculative Fiction
Deep investment in science fiction that takes ideas seriously — especially first-contact, deep time, and civilisational stakes. Prefers biological/sociological SF over tech SF.
Hyperion · Children of Time · Arrival · Blade Runner 2049 · Dark
Arthouse & Slow Cinema
Patient, visually precise filmmaking where mood and implication do the work of dialogue. Tarkovsky, Bergman, and their successors — plus contemporary directors who inherited that tradition.
Stalker · Persona · Mulholland Drive · Parasite · The Master and Margarita
Literary Crime & Moral Complexity
Crime as a vehicle for character study and institutional critique rather than plot mechanics. Breaking Bad, The Wire, No Country for Old Men — the kind of work where the criminal is a mirror.
The Wire · Better Call Saul · No Country for Old Men · True Detective · Slow Horses
Soul, Jazz & Melancholic Atmospherics
Music that sits still and breathes. Nina Simone, Tom Waits, Portishead, Boards of Canada — emotional precision over energy, melancholy as aesthetic choice.
Nina Simone · Tom Waits · Portishead · Boards of Canada · Nick Drake
